# Quick fill-in
This paper provides a comprehensive overview of the principal modelling frameworks used in credit risk measurement and management, with a particular focus on the Vasicek loan portfolio model that underpins the Basel II/III internal ratings‑based (IRB) capital requirements. It begins by explaining the allocation of economic capital through the loss distribution of a credit portfolio, distinguishing expected loss (EL) from unexpected loss (UL) and introducing the concept of portfolio invariance. The discussion then covers structural credit risk models, notably the Merton model, which treats equity as a call option on a firm’s assets and derives default probabilities from asset value dynamics. Reduced‑form (intensity‑based) models are presented as an alternative, where default is modelled as a Poisson event; these models are applied to price contingent convertible capital instruments (CoCos) using the “credit triangle” relation. Finally, the paper addresses counterparty credit risk in over‑the‑counter derivatives markets, detailing credit value adjustment (CVA) and the role of initial and variation margins in mitigating exposure. Throughout, the theoretical exposition is linked to regulatory practice, making the material valuable for both supervisory understanding and practical implementation in financial institutions.
